How to be an Ethical Web Developer

Now that technology is readily available to so many people, it has become more important than ever to consider the implications of design choices, content selection, and cultural backgrounds when creating web programs. It is our job as developers to make sure our products are sufficient to service all kinds of people to the best of our ability, and here is a quick guide to one of the aspects of web design that should be considered to better serve others.

Color Usage

As developers, unless we ourselves have a disability, we often overlook the implications of some of our actions. One of the most obvious barriers to ethical design we face is color choices conducive to colorblindness. The images below provides a wonderful visual to show how people with different types of colorblindness view the same image.

Statistically, the most common type of colorblindness is Deuteranomaly, which is a type of colorblindness that makes green look more like red but is still often identifiable to the person after some scrutiny. In order to best serve potential website users that may be affected by colorblindness, web developers need to be hyperaware of the colors they use on their websites, especially when trying to distinguish between items like in a graph, so they are visible to all. While red and green are contrasting colors to those without colorblindness, they should not be used next to each other or in any combination of a back/foreground.

<style> color:rgb(123, 161, 123); </style>

In CSS, using the color picker tool and changing the RGB values of a color manually is a simple and effective way to make sure the red/green content of any color on your page is not too similar to another.
